## Executive Summary

### Purpose

This document identifies the key stakeholders for the **AgenticEA — MagicDelivery Agent AI Transformation Program**, maps their underlying drivers (motivations, concerns, needs), traces how those drivers manifest into measurable goals, and defines the business outcomes that will satisfy stakeholder objectives. This analysis provides the governance foundation for stakeholder alignment, conflict resolution, and benefits realisation across a 12-to-24 month delivery horizon.

### Key Findings

Nine stakeholder groups with distinct driver profiles have been identified across strategic, financial, operational, compliance, customer, personal, and risk dimensions. The highest-risk tension area is between AI-driven operational efficiency and workforce impact: achieving meaningful call-centre and retail efficiency gains directly intersects with employment considerations for MagicDelivery's 10,000-person workforce under the Fair Work Act. A second significant tension exists between executive pressure for fast market entry and Compliance's requirement for rigorous Privacy Act and APP testing. Stakeholder alignment is assessed as **MEDIUM** — strong strategic alignment exists between Parcel Business and Executive Leadership, but operational and compliance stakeholders require dedicated engagement strategies to prevent resistance or misalignment during rollout.

### Critical Success Factors

- **Executive alignment on AI workforce strategy**: A transparent, board-approved approach to how AI agents augment (rather than replace) operational roles is essential to maintaining workforce trust, union relations, and operational continuity during transition.
- **Privacy-by-design from inception**: Compliance must be embedded into every design and deployment phase, not bolted on pre-launch. Early Privacy Impact Assessments and APP-compliant data handling prevent costly rework and reputational damage.
- **Customer-centric value proof**: The programme must demonstrate tangible customer experience improvements (response time, satisfaction, convenience) within the first 6 months to sustain investment commitment from Executive Leadership and Parcel Business.
- **Phased rollout with clear success gates**: Gradual feature release via the MagicDelivery Mobile App, with measured KPIs at each phase, reduces risk while building stakeholder confidence through visible, incremental results.

### Stakeholder Alignment Score

**Overall Alignment**: MEDIUM

Strong strategic alignment exists between Parcel Business (SD-1), Executive Leadership (SD-9), and Digital Technology (SD-2) around revenue growth, market positioning, and platform capability. Customer drivers (SD-6) are naturally aligned with business outcomes. The primary misalignment clusters are: (1) Operations Staff workload reduction (SD-7) versus perceived job displacement risk, (2) Executive speed-to-market pressure versus Compliance/Legal testing requirements (SD-8), and (3) Feature breadth desired by Parcel Business versus cost control expectations from Executive Leadership. Dedicated influence strategies for Resisters and Fence-sitters are defined in Section 10.

## Stakeholder Drivers Analysis

### SD-1: Parcel Business (Executive Sponsor) — Grow e-commerce revenue and capture market share

**Stakeholder**: Parcel Business GM/VP (Executive Sponsor)

**Driver Category**: STRATEGIC + FINANCIAL

**Driver Statement**: Increase MagicDelivery's share of the international e-commerce parcel market from current baseline through AI-powered services that differentiate the customer experience, reduce fulfilment costs, and drive measurable ROI on AI investment within the first 18 months.

**Context & Background**: MagicDelivery processes over 12 million parcels annually. Competitive pressure from dedicated couriers (Amazon Logistics, Fastways) and self-service platforms is eroding margin. The Parcel Business GM has been directed by the CEO to demonstrate AI as a competitive differentiator, not just a cost tool. The GM's performance scorecard is weighted 40% on revenue growth, 30% on market share, and 30% on cost efficiency. AI agents in the MagicDelivery app represent the highest-ROI digital channel for capturing the growing at-home delivery and click-and-collect segments.

**Driver Intensity**: CRITICAL

**Enablers** (What would help):

- Clear ROI dashboard linking AI features to revenue metrics
- Fast iteration cycles with market feedback loops
- Executive backing to fund pilot-to-production scale-up
- Access to customer behaviour data for agent personalisation

**Blockers** (What would hinder):

- Compliance delays pushing launch past competitive windows
- AI model underperformance damaging brand credibility
- Scope creep diluting focus on revenue-driving features
- Legacy system integration complexity slowing delivery

**Related Stakeholders**:

- SD-9 (Executive Leadership) — shared revenue and competitive positioning goals
- SD-6 (Customers) — experience improvements drive revenue
- SD-2 (Digital Technology) — platform capability enables the strategy

### SD-8: Compliance/Legal — Ensure regulatory compliance and manage risk

**Stakeholder**: Legal & Regulatory Affairs Team

**Driver Category**: COMPLIANCE + RISK

**Driver Statement**: Ensure all AI agent interactions, data processing, and customer-facing behaviours comply with the Privacy Act 1988 (Cth), international Privacy Principles (APPs), ACCC consumer law obligations, and emerging AI governance frameworks, while maintaining defensible governance and audit trails.

**Context & Background**: Compliance/Legal is responsible for ensuring MagicDelivery meets its obligations under the Privacy Act 1988 (including APP 1: open and transparent management of personal information; APP 3: collection of solicited personal information; APP 8: cross-border disclosure), the Competition and Consumer Act 2010 (ACCC oversight of misleading AI-generated content), and potential future AI-specific regulation. The OAIC has been actively enforcing privacy requirements, with penalties up to USD $2.5 million per breach. AI agents introduce unique compliance challenges: training data provenance, hallucination risk (AI providing incorrect information), data collection during agent interactions, cross-border model hosting, and consumer protection obligations for AI-generated advice.

**Driver Intensity**: CRITICAL

**Enablers** (What would help):

- Privacy-by-design embedded in architecture from inception
- Comprehensive Data Protection Impact Assessments (DPIAs)
- Clear audit trails for all AI agent interactions
- Legal sign-off at each design gate
- Engagement with OAIC on AI-specific guidance

**Blockers** (What would hinder):

- Speed-to-market pressure from Executive bypassing compliance reviews
- AI vendors with opaque model training data and hosting arrangements
- Insufficient time allocated for compliance assessments
- Cross-border data transfer complexities with overseas AI platforms
- Regulatory ambiguity around AI-specific obligations

**Related Stakeholders**:

- SD-6 (Customers) — privacy and trust are shared objectives
- SD-2 (Digital Technology) — architecture must embed compliance controls
- SD-9 (Executive Leadership) — board-level risk accountability

### Conflict Analysis

**Competing Drivers**:

- **Speed vs Quality (SD-9 vs SD-8)**: Executive Leadership wants fast market entry to capture competitive advantage, but Compliance/Legal requires thorough Privacy Act testing, DPIAs, and governance sign-offs that extend timelines. These are incompatible because rushing compliance reviews creates regulatory exposure, while thorough reviews delay market positioning.
  - **Resolution Strategy**: Parallel compliance engagement — embed Compliance/Legal in delivery teams from inception rather than gate reviews at the end. Privacy-by-design architecture eliminates the need for post-hoc compliance fixes. Phased rollout allows early feature release (with compliance sign-off) while later features continue compliance review. Budget allocated for dedicated compliance resources within the programme to prevent bottleneck.

- **Cost vs Features (SD-9 vs SD-1)**: Executive Leadership wants cost optimisation (USD $18.5M total programme budget) while Parcel Business wants a comprehensive feature set that differentiates the customer experience. Feature breadth increases cost; cost discipline limits capability.
  - **Resolution Strategy**: Prioritised feature backlog with quarterly ROI review. Phase 1 launches with minimum viable AI capability set (parcel tracking, FAQ agent) to generate early revenue evidence. Phase 2 adds features based on Phase 1 revenue data. Feature prioritisation weighted 60% revenue impact, 30% implementation cost, 10% strategic alignment. Executive Steering Committee approves any budget variance >5%.

- **AI Automation vs Jobs (SD-7 vs SD-1/SD-9)**: Operations Staff require job security and workforce augmentation, while Parcel Business and Executive Leadership need operational efficiency gains that could be interpreted as workforce reduction. The union is actively monitoring AI impact under the Fair Work Act.
  - **Resolution Strategy**: Board-approved AI Augmentation Charter explicitly committing to zero net FTE reduction for 24 months post-launch. AI tools designed for workload augmentation (reducing routine queries, improving tools), not headcount reduction. Union consultation programme with quarterly progress reports. Upskilling investment of USD $1.8M positioned as workforce development, not cost offset. Transparent communication: "AI handles routine queries so you can focus on complex, high-value interactions."

- **Customer Experience vs Privacy (SD-6 vs SD-8)**: Customers want personalised, proactive AI service that anticipates needs, but Compliance requires data minimisation, explicit consent, and transparent data handling. Personalisation requires more data collection; privacy requires less.
  - **Resolution Strategy**: Privacy-by-design with explicit opt-in personalisation. Customers choose their personalisation level (basic tracking vs. proactive recommendations). All data collection points have clear privacy notices and control mechanisms. OAIC-engaged approach to AI-specific data handling guidance. Design principle: minimum data needed for the service function, with progressive enhancement through opt-in features.

**Synergies**:

- **Revenue and Experience Alignment (SD-1 + SD-6)**: Parcel Business revenue growth and Customer desire for better service are naturally synergistic — improved customer experience drives retention, referrals, and revenue. Achieving G-2 (Customer Experience Transformation) directly enables O-1 (Revenue Growth).
- **Platform Reuse and Efficiency (SD-2 + SD-3)**: Digital Technology platform capability building and Program Delivery efficiency are mutually reinforcing — a reusable platform reduces future delivery effort and cost. Achieving G-5 (Platform Capability) enables both SD-2 and SD-3 objectives.
- **Privacy and Trust (SD-8 + SD-6)**: Compliance/Legal privacy requirements and Customer trust requirements are aligned — customers who trust data handling are more likely to engage with AI features. Achieving G-4 (Privacy Compliance) enables G-2 (Customer Experience) by removing trust barriers.
- **Workforce and Customer Experience (SD-7 + SD-6)**: Better-staffed, better-equipped operations teams deliver better customer interactions. AI reducing routine workload allows staff to focus on high-value interactions, improving both staff satisfaction and customer experience.
